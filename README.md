# WNBA Draft Board Model

A data-driven WNBA draft tool that ranks NCAA women's basketball players by their fit for specific WNBA teams. Built by Derek Yee & Vedanth Chamala (team needs model) and Evan & Jenna (player archetypes & ranking).

---

## What It Does

For each WNBA team on the clock, the model answers: **"Which available NCAA players best address this team's specific weaknesses?"**

It combines two signals:
- **WNBA Readiness Score** — how good is this player overall, adjusted for competition?
- **Team Fit Score** — how well do this player's strengths address what this specific team lacks?

The final ranking is `55% readiness + 45% fit` (adjustable in the app).

## How the Model Works

### Step 1 — Multi-Year Scraping
`01_scrape_multi_year.py` extends the original single-year scraper to loop over 2022–2025, hitting [sports-reference.com](https://www.sports-reference.com/cbb/) for all 31 conferences. Output is one CSV per year.

Both scripts are checkpoint-safe: they skip years (step 1) or players (step 2) whose output files already exist, so you can safely interrupt and resume.

### Step 2 — Physical Measurables
`02_scrape_measurables.py` fetches each player's profile page to extract height and weight. These feed into the Ridge model as optional features — players without measurables are still scored via median imputation.

### Step 3 — Multi-Year Feature Aggregation
`03_build_features.py` collapses the per-year data into one row per player using **exponential recency weighting combined with minutes weighting**:

```
weight = exp(0.5 × (year − 2022)) × minutes_played
```

This means a player's 2025 season counts ~4.5× more than their 2022 season, and a 35 mpg season counts more than a 5 mpg cameo. The script also computes:

- **Season trend** — slope of BPM and PPG over time (rewards improving players)
- **Derived features** — AST/TOV ratio, three-point rate, inside scoring composite, defensive composite
- **Transfer handling** — when a player appears on multiple teams in one year, the highest-MPG row is kept

For single-year data (the fallback), each player gets one row with no trend signal.

### Step 4 — Ridge Regression Readiness Score
`04_ridge_model.py` trains a Ridge regression model to output a 0–100 **WNBA readiness score**.

**Why Ridge?** Basketball stats are highly collinear — PPG, USG%, and FGA all move together. Ordinary least squares would assign extreme coefficients to correlated features. Ridge shrinks those coefficients proportionally, producing a more stable composite that doesn't over-weight any single stat.

**Target variable** — since we don't have WNBA outcome labels for current prospects, the target is a competition-adjusted college quality composite:

```
raw_quality = 0.30 × z(ws/40) + 0.30 × z(BPM) + 0.20 × z(PER) + 0.20 × z(TS%)
competition_multiplier = clip(1 + 0.25 × z(adj_opp_win_pct), 0.75, 1.25)
target = raw_quality × competition_multiplier
```

A player who puts up elite advanced numbers in the SEC gets a higher target than an identically-raw player from a weaker conference.

The Ridge model then learns which **raw counting stats** predict this composite — handling collinearity properly — and outputs a single score for any new player.

> **Validation note:** CV R² is high (~0.96) because the target is derived from input features. The meaningful validation is rank-order stability across folds (Kendall's τ), which should be > 0.85. The score is best interpreted as a relative ranking, not an absolute prediction.

**Top features by Ridge coefficient:**

| Feature | Direction | Interpretation |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| PER | + | Catch-all efficiency |
| dbpm | + | Defensive impact |
| obpm | + | Offensive impact |
| ts_pct | + | Shooting quality |
| def_composite | + | Blocks + steals + DWS |

### Step 5 — Player–Team Fit Score
`05_fit_scores.py` bridges WNBA team weaknesses (computed in `WNBA_needs_model.ipynb`) to NCAA player strengths.

**WNBA team needs** come from the notebook: for each of 12 statistical features most correlated with winning (NRtg, MOV, SRS, etc.), each team gets a `weighted_deficit` — how far below league average they are, scaled by that stat's importance to winning.

**WNBA → NCAA stat mapping** connects each team-level WNBA need to the individual NCAA stats that most directly address it:

| WNBA Team Need | NCAA Player Stats Used | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| `PTS_per_100` | pts_per_g, usg_pct, obpm | High |
| `AST_per_100` | ast_per_g, ast_pct | High |
| `DRB_per_100` | dreb_per_g, dreb_pct, treb_pct | High |
| `TS.` | ts_pct, efg_pct | High |
| `opp_FG%_per_game` | blk_per_g, blk_pct, dbpm | Medium (proxy) |
| `opp_3FG%_per_game` | stl_per_g, stl_pct, dbpm | Medium (proxy) |

> **Note on defensive stats:** WNBA opponent stats (`opp_FG%`, `Opp_eFG`, etc.) are team-level defensive metrics with no clean individual NCAA equivalent. The mappings above use the best available proxies (blocks, steals, dbpm). Fit scores for teams with primarily defensive needs will be noisier than for teams with offensive needs.

**Fit score formula:**
```
fit_score(player, team) = Σ over needs:
    team_deficit[need] × Σ (player_z[ncaa_stat] × stat_weight × confidence)
```

**Final combined score:**
```
total_score = 0.55 × z(readiness_score) + 0.45 × z(fit_score)
```

The 55/45 split is a starting point — the React app exposes a slider to adjust it.

### Player Archetypes
Players are clustered into 4 archetypes by Team 2 (Evan & Jenna) using KMeans on the feature set. These archetypes are used for filtering and as a secondary signal in the fit score:

| Cluster | Archetype | Key stats |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| 2 | **Primary Creator** ⚡ | High USG%, PPG, shot creation |
| 1 | **Balanced Contributor** ⚖️ | Moderate across all categories |
| 3 | **Interior Defender** 🛡 | High BLK, REB, interior anchoring |
| 0 | **Support Player** 🔧 | High AST%, low USG%, role player |

## The React Webapp

The app has three views:

**Team Grid** — all 13 WNBA teams with their top archetype needs displayed.

**Team View** — click any team to see:
- Their top statistical weaknesses (from the needs model)
- All available players ranked by fit score for that team
- Filters by archetype and position
- Click a player row to expand detailed stats (including Readiness score)
- "Draft" button to mark a player as taken (persisted to localStorage)

**Draft Board** — log of all drafted players with undo support.

### Fit score display
When `prepare_data.py` has been run with the full pipeline output, the "Fit" column in the player table uses the pre-computed model score (0–100). Without the pipeline, it falls back to an archetype-based formula using `archetype_score` and `rank_in_archetype`.

## Key Design Decisions

**Why exponential weighting and not just most recent season?** A freshman's sophomore improvement trajectory is signal. A player who was great for 3 years then had an injury year should not be penalized as heavily. Recency weighting captures both.

**Why Ridge and not Lasso or a tree model?** Lasso zeroes out correlated features entirely, which would drop either PPG or USG% — both carry real signal. Trees overfit on small samples. Ridge finds a smooth, stable compromise that handles collinearity well and generalizes to new players.

**Why is the fit score 45% of the total?** A player who perfectly addresses one team's needs but is a mediocre prospect overall is still a bad pick. The readiness score (55%) prevents the model from recommending weak players just because their archetype fits. The ratio is tunable.

**Why use `weighted_deficit` from the notebook rather than raw deficits?** The weighted deficit already incorporates how important each stat is to winning (via the correlation weights). This means a team's rebounding deficit is automatically discounted if rebounding is less predictive of wins than scoring efficiency.
